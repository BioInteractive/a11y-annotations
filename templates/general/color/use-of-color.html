Use of Color

Color must not be used as the only visual means of conveying information, indicating an action, prompting a response, or distinguishing a visual element.

  • Understand Color Accessibility: Familiarize yourself with WCAG 2.1 Guidelines to help understand that color should not be the only means of conveying information.
  • Provide Alternative Indicators: When using color to convey information (like errors, success, links), always include an alternative indicator. This could be a text label, an icon, underlining, or patterns.
  • Use Descriptive Labels and Icons: Accompany color cues with clear text labels or descriptive icons, especially for important actions like error messages, success notifications, or call-to-action buttons.
  • Annotate Color Usage: When annotating your designs, explicitly mention how color is used and what additional indicators are provided for accessibility.
  • Interactive Elements: For interactive elements like links or buttons, use more than color to indicate interactivity. Include underlines, different font styles or borders, hover states and/or focus states.
  • Feedback and Error States: Clearly indicate error states or feedback messages with text descriptions and/or icons alongside color changes.

  • Identify Color-Reliant Information: Examine the design and identify any instances where color is used to convey information or distinguish visual elements. This may include things like:
    • Using red text and/or borders to indicate errors.
    • Using green text and/or borders to indicate success.
    • Using color-coded icons or labels to represent different categories or statuses.
    • Using color alone to indicate a link in a block of text.
  • Check for Redundant Cues: Ensure that information or distinctions made through color are also conveyed using additional visual cues, such as text labels, icons, patterns, or shapes. This provides redundancy and ensures that people who cannot perceive color can still understand the content.

Implement content without relying solely on color to convey information. Non-color indicators may include:

  • Text Labels: Use clear and descriptive text labels alongside color indicators.
  • Icons and Symbols: Incorporate universally recognized icons or symbols to convey information.
  • Texture or Patterns: Apply different textures or patterns to elements that need to be distinguished from one another.
  • Shape: Use distinct shapes to signify different statuses or categories.
  • Size: Alter the size of elements to indicate their importance or to differentiate between items.
  • Positioning: Place elements strategically to suggest a relationship or to differentiate them.
  • Typography: Employ bold text, different font styles, or underlining to highlight information.
  • Contrast: Increase contrast with background elements, not just with color, but also with lightness or darkness with a minimum of a 3:1 contrast ratio.

These practices ensure content is accessible to everyone, including users with color vision deficiencies.
