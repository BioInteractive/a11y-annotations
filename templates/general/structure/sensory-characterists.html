Sensory Characteristics

1.3.3 Sensory Characteristics ensures that instructions for interacting with content do not rely solely on sensory characteristics such as shape, size, visual location, orientation, or sound. This success criterion requires that:

  • Instructions describe elements using text-based descriptions rather than relying solely on sensory characteristics (e.g., “Click the search icon labeled with a magnifying glass” instead of “Click the round icon”).
  • Shape, color, size, or other sensory information should be complemented with textual or programmatically determinable descriptions.
  • Instructions must ensure equal access for users who rely on assistive technologies or have difficulty perceiving sensory characteristics.

Ensuring that instructions are not based solely on sensory characteristics is critical for users with vision impairments, cognitive disabilities, or users who rely on screen readers or alternative interaction methods.

Examples:

  • Prefer: “Activate the search icon labeled with a magnifying glass.”
  • Avoid: “Click the round icon.”
  • Prefer: “Press the green button labeled Submit.”
  • Avoid: “Press the green button.”

Common Failures:

  • Providing instructions that rely solely on shapes or locations (e.g., “Select the square in the top-right corner”).
  • Using color as the only means of conveying information (e.g., “Click the blue button”).
  • Failing to include accessible names or programmatically determinable labels.

Techniques:

  • Use descriptive text that clearly explains how to interact with elements.
  • Complement sensory information (e.g., shape, size) with textual descriptions or accessible labels.
  • Ensure that all interactive elements are programmatically determinable and provide meaningful names for screen readers.

Designing for Sensory Accessibility

  • Complement Sensory Cues: In your designs, ensure that all sensory information (e.g., shape, color, size, orientation) is complemented with textual descriptions or alternative icons.
  • Clear Instructional Design: Create prototypes that clearly differentiate interactive elements through both visual and text-based cues.
  • Annotate Sensory Elements: Label and annotate design components that rely on sensory characteristics, specifying alternative cues for development.

  1. Review Design Artifacts: Examine Figma prototypes to ensure that sensory-based instructions are complemented with text or icon alternatives.
  2. Annotate Missing Alternatives: Document any instances where instructions rely solely on sensory cues, noting where additional information is needed.
  3. Confirm Consistency: Verify that alternative cues are consistently applied across all similar design elements.

Implementing Sensory Characteristics

  • Use Descriptive Instructions: Ensure that instructions do not rely solely on sensory cues like shape, size, color, or location. For example, use “Activate the search icon labeled with a magnifying glass” instead of “Click the round icon.”
  • Complement Visual Cues with Text: Provide textual or programmatically determinable descriptions to supplement sensory characteristics.
  • Ensure Accessible Names: Use semantic HTML and ARIA attributes to provide accessible names and labels for interactive elements.
  • Avoid Ambiguity: Validate that users can identify interactive elements through both sensory and textual cues.
  • Avoid Solely Color-Based Instructions: Do not rely on color alone; use additional markers such as icons or text to differentiate actions.

Implementing these practices ensures that sensory-based instructions are accessible to users with visual, cognitive, or perceptual disabilities.
