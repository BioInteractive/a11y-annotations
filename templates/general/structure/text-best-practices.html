Best Practices for Text Readability and Accessibility

Best Practices

Ensuring text is readable and accessible is crucial for making content inclusive for everyone, including individuals with disabilities. Here are some key practices to keep in mind:

Use Clear and Simple Language

  • Plain Language: Use straightforward language that is easy to understand. Avoid jargon and complex sentences.
  • Active Voice: Prefer active voice over passive voice to make sentences clearer.

Choose Readable Fonts

  • Sans-Serif Fonts: Fonts like Arial, Verdana, and Helvetica are generally easier to read on screens.
  • Font Size: Ensure a minimum font size of 16px for body text to enhance readability.

Provide Adequate Spacing

  • Line Height: Use a line height (line spacing) of at least 1.5 times the font size.
  • Paragraph Spacing: Leave sufficient space between paragraphs to break up text and make it easier to scan.

Avoid All Caps and Italics

  • All Caps: Text in all capital letters can be harder to read and may be interpreted as shouting.
  • Italics: Use italics sparingly as they can be difficult to read, especially for individuals with dyslexia.

Use Headings and Lists

  • Headings: Use headings (H1, H2, H3, etc.) to structure your content logically. This helps screen readers and improves navigation. [1.3.1 Info and Relationships]
  • Lists: Use bullet points or numbered lists to break down information into digestible chunks.

Ensure Sufficient Contrast

  • Color Contrast: Make sure there is a high contrast between text and background colors. This helps people with visual impairments or color blindness. Tools like Deque's color contrast analyzer can be very helpful. [1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum)]

Provide Text Alternatives

  • Alt Text: Always provide descriptive alt text for images to ensure that screen readers can convey the information to users who are blind or have low vision. [1.1.1 Non-text Content]
  • Captions and Transcripts: For audio and video content, provide captions and transcripts. [1.2.1 Audio-only and Video-only (Prerecorded)]

Test with Assistive Technologies

  • Screen Readers: Test your content with screen readers to ensure it is navigable and understandable. [4.1.2 Name, Role, Value]
  • Keyboard Navigation: Ensure that all interactive elements can be accessed and used with a keyboard. [2.1.1 Keyboard]
