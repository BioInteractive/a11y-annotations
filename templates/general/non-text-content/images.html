Images

1.1.1 Non-text Content All meaningful non-text content (images, video, audio, SVGs, icons and other content that isn't conveyed by text) needs to have alt text that provides the same information found in the content.

Every <img> must have an alt attribute.

Meaningful Image Example

If the image is informational/meaningful, set the alt equal to a descriptive alternative for that image. Alt text for an image MUST describe the content and function of the image succinctly, including relevant details or context that are essential to understanding the webpage.

Avoid using generic strings like photo, image, or icon as alt values, as they don't communicate valuable content to the user. Be as descriptive as possible.

Decorative Image Example

If the image is decorative or redundant to adjacent text, set alt="", which conveys to assistive technology users that the image isn't necessary for understanding the page. Purely decorative content (for example, a dividing line between a navigation bar and content) does not require alternative text.

Complex Image Example

Photosynthesis Process Illustration of the process of photosynthesis in a tree. The sun is depicted shining on the tree, which uses the energy to convert water absorbed from the ground and carbon dioxide from the air into carbohydrates and oxygen. The transformation is shown with arrows: water molecules and carbon dioxide entering the tree, and carbohydrates, depicted as clusters of colorful atoms, along with oxygen molecules, are shown as the output. This visual representation highlights the tree's role in producing vital oxygen and storing energy in the form of carbohydrates through photosynthesis.

Images that are too complex to be fully described in a short text alternative MUST have their purpose described using alt text. Equivalent text alternatives to complex images MUST be provided in context or on a separate page (linked and/or referenced via aria-describedby property).

As a Designer using Figma, your role in ensuring accessibility for images begins with thoughtful planning and documentation:

  • Choice of Images: You're free to choose images that best suit the design needs, but be mindful about the use of text within images and the contrast in images.
  • Documentation: For each image you decide to include in the design, document its purpose and whether it's decorative or meaningful. This documentation will be invaluable for developers and content authors when they assign alternative text or decide on the appropriate ARIA attributes.
  • Consult with Content Leads: For complex or meaningful images, work closely with content leads or authors to understand the context and the message the image conveys. This collaboration ensures that appropriate and accurate alternative text can be developed, enhancing accessibility.
  • Be Mindful of Images of Text: Use text within images sparingly and only when necessary. If the image of text is essential, ensure that it's legible and meets color contrast requirements. Always consider how the text will be translated into alternative text for assistive technologies.

By incorporating these practices into your workflow, you contribute significantly to creating designs that are accessible and inclusive from the start.

Meaningful Images

Meaningful images are those that convey important information or functionality to users. These images play a significant role in providing context, enhancing understanding, or facilitating user interactions on a web page. Examples of meaningful images include:

  • Graphs and charts that illustrate data and statistics.
  • Icons representing actionable elements like buttons, links, or controls.
  • Images depicting products, people, or objects relevant to the content.
  • Illustrations supporting the textual content to explain concepts or processes.

Decorative Images

Decorative images are images that are used purely for aesthetic or decorative purposes and does not convey meaningful content or information. Decorative images are typically added to enhance the visual design of a web page but do not serve a functional role in conveying information to users. A few examples include:

  • Ornamental borders used to create decorative borders around content or sections of a webpage.
  • Background images used in the background of a webpage to create a specific ambiance or atmosphere.
  • Background textures to enhance the visual appearance of a webpage.
  • Icons or graphics used purely for visual embellishment and not representing actionable elements.

Additional annotations for SVGs and Icons

SVGs

  • Meaningful SVG must have a <title> as the first child with a unique IDREF.
  • The <svg> must then have an aria-labelledby="IDREF"
  • Decorative SVG must have aria-hidden="true" so it will be ignored by assistive technologies.

Icons

  • Meaningful Icon must have an accessible name from an aria-label, aria-labelledby or sr-only text.
  • Decorative Icon must have aria-hidden="true" so it will be ignored by assistive technologies.

Implementing Images for Accessibility

Proper implementation of images is crucial for web accessibility, adhering to WCAG 2.1 standards, particularly for non-text content and images of text:

  • Alt Text for Images: Provide meaningful alternative (alt) text for images that convey information. Alt text should succinctly describe the image's content or function.
  • SVGs: For meaningful SVGs, use a <title> tag immediately after the SVG tag to provide an accessible name, and reference it with aria-labelledby. Decorative SVGs should have an aria-hidden="true" attribute to hide them from assistive technologies.
  • Icons: Decorative icons should be placed in a container with aria-hidden="true" to ensure they are not announced by screen readers. Meaningful icons require an accessible name, provided via aria-label or visually hidden text that can be translated into other languages for broader accessibility.
  • Images of Text: Avoid using images of text unless absolutely necessary. If images of text are used, ensure they meet contrast requirements and provide an accessible alternative for the text content.
  • Testing and Validation: Use accessibility testing tools to validate the presence of alt text and appropriate use of ARIA attributes for images, SVGs, and icons.

Following these guidelines ensures that all users, including those using assistive technologies, can fully engage with your web content.
