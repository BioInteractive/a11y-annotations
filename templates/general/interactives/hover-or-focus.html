Content on Hover or Focus

1.4.13 Content on Hover or Focus is a guideline that ensures any additional content that appears when a user hovers over or focuses on an element is accessible to all users.

  • Dismissible: There must be a way to close the additional content without moving the pointer or keyboard focus, unless the additional content is an error message or does not block or replace other content. This helps users remove unnecessary content that might be obstructing their view or interaction.
  • Hoverable: If the additional content appears when hovering, the user must be able to move their pointer over this content without it disappearing. This ensures that users can interact with the additional content without it vanishing unexpectedly.
  • Persistent: The additional content should remain visible until the hover or focus is removed, the user dismisses it, or the information is no longer relevant. This ensures a stable and predictable experience for users interacting with dynamic content.

Exception

If the visual presentation of the additional content is controlled by the browser and not modified by the author, it is exempt from this requirement.

  • Examples of browser-controlled additional content include tooltips created using the HTML title attribute.
  • Custom tooltips, sub-menus, and other non-modal popups that appear on hover and focus are examples of additional content that must comply with this criterion.

Design Considerations for Hover and Focus Elements in Figma

It is advisable to avoid having content revealed on hover or focus, as this removes the user's choice to trigger the content and can lead to accessibility issues. Instead, design these elements to be triggered by click and key press for both mouse and keyboard users. However, if content authors specifically request content to appear on hover and focus, ensure they are designed to be dismissible, hoverable, and persistent, showing the different states in your design.

  • Show Different States: Provide examples of different states for interactive elements in your Figma designs. Clearly show what happens when an element gains focus, hover, or is in its default state. This helps users understand how the element behaves in various situations.
  • Document User Flows: Clearly document user flows in your design to specify how focus and hover states should transition between elements. This helps ensure that focus remains stable and predictable for users.
  • Explicit User Control: Highlight in your designs that actions like navigation or form submissions require explicit user actions, such as clicking a button or pressing a key, rather than merely focusing on or hovering over an element.
  • Visual Indicators for Hover and Focus: Ensure all focusable elements have clear visual indicators when they receive focus or hover. Use Figma's prototyping features to show how these indicators look and behave, making it easy for users to see where their focus or hover is.
  • Dismissible Elements: Design dismissible elements with clear close buttons or other mechanisms that allow users to easily dismiss the additional content without moving focus or hover away from the triggering element.
  • Hoverable Elements: Ensure that elements which reveal additional content on hover allow users to move their pointer over the additional content without it disappearing. This can be demonstrated by designing hoverable states and showing how the user can interact with the additional content.
  • Persistent Elements: Design additional content to remain visible until the hover or focus is removed, the user dismisses it, or the information is no longer relevant. Provide visual feedback for these states in your Figma designs.

By clearly documenting hover and focus behavior and providing detailed examples in your Figma designs, you help ensure that developers can implement accessible and operable web content, aligning with WCAG 1.4.13 Content on Hover or Focus criteria.

  1. Visual Inspection: Carefully examine the Figma designs, focusing on the layout and order of interactive elements. Ensure that the sequence in which elements are presented is logical and meaningful.
  2. Annotation for Dismissibility: Annotate designs to indicate how users can dismiss additional content that appears on hover or focus. Ensure there are clear mechanisms such as close buttons.
  3. Hover and Focus Indicators: Include annotations or notes within your Figma design that describe the visual indicators for elements that appear on hover or focus. Ensure these indicators are clear and intuitive.
  4. Persistent Content Annotation: Annotate designs to show how additional content remains visible until the hover or focus is removed, the user dismisses it, or the information becomes irrelevant.
  5. Preparing for Developer Hand-off: Provide additional annotations outlining the behavior of content that appears on hover or focus. This serves as a reference for developers during implementation.
  6. Focus and Hover State Documentation: Document how focus and hover states should behave, ensuring there are no automatic context changes. Describe how users can interact with and dismiss additional content.

Content on Hover or Focus

When receiving and then removing pointer hover or keyboard focus triggers additional content to become visible and then hidden, ensure the following:

  • Provide a mechanism to dismiss the additional content without moving pointer hover or keyboard focus, unless the additional content communicates an input error or does not obscure or replace other content.
  • If pointer hover can trigger the additional content, ensure that the pointer can be moved over the additional content without it disappearing.
  • Ensure the additional content remains visible until the hover or focus trigger is removed, the user dismisses it, or its information is no longer valid.

Implementing Content on Hover or Focus Accessibility

  • Dismissable Content: Ensure that any additional content triggered by focus or hover can be dismissed by the user without moving focus or hover, unless it communicates an input error or does not obscure other content.
  • Hoverable Content: If additional content can be triggered by hover, make sure the pointer can move over the additional content without it disappearing.
  • Persistent Content: Ensure that the additional content remains visible until the focus or hover trigger is removed, the user dismisses it, or its information is no longer valid.
  • ARIA Roles and Attributes: Use appropriate ARIA roles and attributes to identify and manage the visibility of additional content. For example, use aria-live to announce changes and aria-hidden to hide content from assistive technologies when it is not visible.
  • Focus Management: Ensure that focus is not unintentionally moved when additional content appears. Focus should remain on the triggering element unless a user action explicitly moves it.
  • Accessible Dismiss Mechanisms: Provide accessible ways for users to dismiss additional content. This can include a close button that is focusable and keyboard operable.
  • Visual and Non-Visual Feedback: Provide both visual and non-visual feedback for the appearance and disappearance of additional content. Use ARIA live regions to alert screen reader users to the changes.
  • Testing and Validation: Regularly test your application to ensure that additional content triggered by focus or hover behaves as expected. Use keyboard-only navigation to verify that all interactive elements remain accessible and that focus order is logical and intuitive.
  • Consistent Behavior: Ensure that the behavior of additional content is consistent across all devices and input methods. This includes testing with both mouse and keyboard to ensure functionality is maintained.

By ensuring that hover and focus-triggered content is managed properly, developers can create accessible and user-friendly web content that aligns with WCAG 2.1 criterion 1.4.13 for Content on Hover or Focus.
