Carousel

Component Accessibility Considerations

HHMI has a policy to avoid using carousels in web design due to their known accessibility issues. Carousels often fail to meet several WCAG requirements and present challenges for many users, including those with disabilities. Below, we list the most common accessibility issues with carousels and provide alternative methods for presenting content typically found in carousels.

Common Accessibility Issues with Carousels

Carousels are a popular design element, but they often come with significant accessibility challenges, including:

  • Inconsistent Navigation: Carousels can make navigation difficult for keyboard and screen reader users. Users must be able to pause, stop, or navigate the carousel using keyboard controls, which is often not implemented correctly. [W3C: Carousels]
  • Focus Order Issues: Moving content in carousels can disrupt the logical focus order, making it confusing for keyboard and screen reader users to navigate through the content. [WCAG 2.4.3 Focus Order]
  • Time Constraints: Auto-rotating carousels can move too quickly for users to read and interact with the content, creating a poor user experience. [WCAG 2.2.1 Timing Adjustable]
  • Screen Reader Announcements: Screen readers may not properly announce the content of each slide, leading to incomplete information being conveyed to users. [WCAG 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value]
  • Content Visibility: Important content can be hidden in slides that are not currently visible, which can be problematic for users who rely on screen magnification. [Resonator: 5 Reasons Carousels are Bad UX]

Alternative Ways to Present Carousel Content

Instead of using carousels, consider these accessible alternatives to present content:

  • Static Images and Content: Display all images and content statically on the page, allowing users to scroll through at their own pace. This approach avoids the pitfalls of auto-rotating carousels and ensures that all content is always visible.
  • Tabbed Interfaces: Use tabs to organize content into different sections that users can navigate through easily. Tabs are generally more accessible and provide a clearer structure for content. [W3C: Tabs]
  • Collapsible Sections: Use expandable and collapsible sections to organize content. This allows users to expand only the sections they are interested in, providing a more accessible way to navigate through the content. [W3C: Accordions]
  • Manual Slideshows: If a slideshow is necessary, ensure it is fully controllable by the user, with accessible buttons for navigation, play, pause, and a clear indication of the current slide. [W3C: Sliders]
