Data Visualization

Accessible data visualizations are designed to ensure that all users, including those with disabilities, can understand and interact with the visual data effectively. Below are the WCAG success criteria specific to data visualizations, ensuring they are accessible and usable for everyone.

  • Accessible Name: Data visualizations must have an accessible name that clearly describes their function. This can be achieved using aria-labelledby or aria-label. 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value
  • Color Contrast (Text): Ensure that all text in the data visualization has sufficient color contrast against its background to be readable by users with visual impairments. 1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum)
  • Color Contrast (Graphical Elements): Ensure that graphical elements in the data visualization, such as bars and lines, have sufficient color contrast against their background to be distinguishable by users with visual impairments. 1.4.11 Non-text Contrast
  • Use of Color: Do not rely solely on color to differentiate elements in a data visualization. Use text labels, patterns, or shapes in addition to color to ensure information is accessible to users who cannot perceive color differences. 1.4.1 Use of Color
  • Text Descriptions: Provide alternative text descriptions for visualizations to convey the data to users who rely on screen readers. 1.1.1 Non-text Content

Patterns of Zoonotic Disease

Detailed Description of the Data Visualization

This image is a complex data visualization composed of six world maps, each representing the distribution of species with zoonoses across different mammalian orders. The six maps are organized in three rows. Each map includes a color gradient legend on the right side, which indicates the number of species with zoonoses, ranging from white (0 species) to purple (25 species).

  • Row One:
    • Carnivora: This map shows a high concentration of zoonotic species in areas including North America, Europe, and parts of Asia, highlighted in red and orange shades. Other regions, such as South America and Africa, show moderate concentrations in yellow shades.
    • Chiroptera: This map displays the highest concentrations in Central and South America, parts of Africa, and Southeast Asia, indicated by purple and red shades.
  • Row Two:
    • Primates: The map for primates shows fewer zoonotic species compared to others, with significant concentrations in Central Africa and parts of South America, represented by yellow shades.
    • Rodentia: This map highlights significant concentrations across much of North America, Europe, and Asia, indicated by red and orange shades, with moderate concentrations in South America and Africa.
  • Row Three:
    • Soricomorpha: The distribution is more limited, with moderate concentrations in North America and parts of Asia, indicated by yellow shades.
    • Ungulates: This map shows widespread moderate concentrations across North America, Europe, and Asia, with additional concentrations in South America and Africa, represented by yellow shades.

The color gradient legend to the right of the maps provides a scale from 0 to 25 species, with white indicating areas with no species and purple indicating areas with up to 25 species with zoonoses. The maps have latitude and longitude lines marked for geographic reference, with latitude ranging from -50 to 50 and longitude ranging from -100 to 100.

  • Color Contrast: Ensure all elements in the data visualizations, including text and graphical components, have a high color contrast ratio (at least 4.5:1) against the background. Utilize Figma plugins like "Contrast" to verify adequate color contrast.
  • Non-reliance on Color Alone: Do not rely solely on color to differentiate data. Use patterns, shapes, or text labels in addition to color. In Figma, apply different styles and layers to achieve this.
  • Text Labels: Include clear and concise text labels for all significant parts of the visualization, such as titles, legends, and data points. Ensure text layers are properly named and organized in Figma.
  • Alternative Text: Provide alternative text descriptions for data visualizations to make them accessible to screen reader users. This can be added in the design documentation or as annotations in Figma.
  • Interactive Elements: If the visualization includes interactive elements, ensure they are operable via keyboard and provide visible focus indicators. Use Figma’s prototyping tools to simulate interactions.
  • Responsive Design: Design visualizations to be responsive and adapt to various screen sizes. Use Figma’s auto-layout feature to create components that adjust dynamically.
  • Supplemental Formats: Consider providing data in multiple formats, such as tables or lists, to cater to different user needs. Include these formats in the design documentation.
  • Content Approval: Consult with content leads to ensure that any supplemental formats or alternative text is approved by them.

When auditing a data visualization component within a Figma design, it is crucial to review the documentation provided by content leads and the implementation by designers for consistency and alignment with accessibility standards.

During the audit of the data visualization component in Figma, consider the following accessibility aspects to flag any issues for the designer's review:

  • Color Contrast: Review the data visualization for sufficient color contrast against the background to ensure visibility for users with visual impairments.

  • Text Labels: Verify that all significant parts of the visualization, such as titles, legends, and data points, are clearly labeled with concise text.
  • Alternative Text: Ensure that alternative text descriptions are provided for the visualization to make it accessible to screen reader users.

Data Visualization Accessibility Checklist

  • Must have sufficient color contrast (at least 4.5:1) for all text and graphical elements.
  • Must use patterns, shapes, or text labels in addition to color to differentiate data.
  • Must have clear and concise text labels for all significant parts of the visualization (titles, legends, data points).
  • Must provide alternative text descriptions for screen reader accessibility.
  • Must be designed to be responsive and adapt to various screen sizes.
  • Should provide data in multiple formats (e.g., tables or lists).

  • Accessible Name: Ensure the data visualization has an accessible name that clearly describes its function. Use aria-labelledby or aria-label to provide this information.
  • Color Contrast: Verify that all text and graphical elements in the data visualization have sufficient color contrast (at least 4.5:1) against the background to ensure visibility and readability for users with visual impairments.
  • Non-reliance on Color Alone: Do not rely solely on color to differentiate elements in the data visualization. Use patterns, shapes, or text labels to convey information to users who cannot perceive color differences.
  • Text Descriptions: Provide alternative text descriptions for visualizations to convey the data to users who rely on screen readers. Use the aria-describedby attribute to associate the visualization with its description.
  • Responsive Design: Ensure the data visualization is designed to be responsive and adapts to various screen sizes. Use CSS media queries and scalable vector graphics (SVG) to achieve this.
  • ARIA Roles and Properties: Properly implement ARIA roles and properties to ensure that the data visualization is recognized and operable by assistive technologies. Use roles like img and properties like aria-describedby.
  • Keyboard Navigation: Ensure that all interactive elements within the data visualization are keyboard accessible. Implement focus indicators to help users navigate.
